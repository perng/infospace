## What is a cell?
*Gallery of Natural History · West Wall — Definition*

### What Is a Cell?

Every living thing is built from cells: self-contained workshops that read instructions, build molecular machines, and copy themselves. Your body runs about 36 trillion of them. A bacterium makes do with one.

The word came before the science. When Robert Hooke examined cork in 1665, the tiny chambers reminded him of monks' rooms in a monastery — cellulae.

**Speaker notes:** 36 trillion cells in a human body. Land on the etymology: Hooke named them after monastery rooms before anyone knew what they did.

## The microscopist
*Gallery of Natural History · East Wall — The Microscopist*

*Image — The Microscopist, c. 1665 (imagined)*: An oil portrait in seventeenth-century style of a natural philosopher beside a brass microscope. No verified portrait of Robert Hooke survives; this painting imagines the man behind Micrographia.

**Speaker notes:** Fun fact: no verified portrait of Hooke survives — possibly thanks to his feud with Newton. This painting is an imagination, and the plaque says so.

## Cork, 1665
*Gallery of Natural History · West Wall — Cork Mural*

*Image — Cork, as Hooke drew it — the first cells ever named*: An engraving-style illustration of a thin slice of cork seen through an early microscope, showing the honeycomb of empty cell walls that Hooke described in Micrographia, 1665.

**Speaker notes:** What Hooke actually saw: dead cell walls in cork, a honeycomb of emptiness. The name stuck to the living thing later.

## Hooke's own words
*Gallery of Natural History · The Hooke Quote*

### Robert Hooke, Micrographia, 1665

“…I could exceedingly plainly perceive it to be all perforated and porous, much like a Honey-comb… these pores, or cells, were indeed the first microscopical pores I ever saw.”

**Speaker notes:** Read the quote aloud, slowly. 'The first microscopical pores I ever saw' — the moment a word became a science.

## The brass threshold
*Gallery of Natural History · The Brass Microscope*

### The Brass Threshold

Every great museum keeps one door it rarely opens. Ours is made of glass and light.

Step up to the eyepiece. The next wing of this gallery is ten thousand times smaller than the last.

**Speaker notes:** Build anticipation. Ask the room: what is the one exhibit a natural history museum can never put behind glass? The inside of the glass itself. Then advance — through the lens.

## Through the lens
*Inside the Cell · Cytoplasm Arrival*

### Inside the Cell

You are now twenty-five micrometres tall. The sea around you is cytoplasm. The lights in the distance are organelles — the cell's working organs.

**Speaker notes:** Pause here. Let the scale shift sink in: we are now 10,000x smaller. The fog is cytoplasm; everything that glows is machinery.

## The nucleus
*Inside the Cell · Specimen Plate — Nucleus*

### The Nucleus

The cell's library. Two metres of DNA, wound and folded into a sphere a few millionths of a metre wide.

Plans are read here and sent out as messenger RNA. The library lends copies — never originals.

**Speaker notes:** Two metres of DNA in every nucleus. If the nucleus were this room, the DNA would be a thread 800 km long, and it still would not tangle.

## The power stations
*Inside the Cell · Hologram — Energy Yield*

### ATP per Molecule of Glucose

Energy yield per molecule of glucose: glycolysis alone produces 2 ATP; with mitochondrial respiration the cell harvests about 32 ATP — a sixteen-fold gain.

| | |
| --- | --- |
| Glycolysis alone | 2 ATP |
| With mitochondria | 32 ATP |

**Speaker notes:** The 16x energy gain is why complex life waited two billion years for the mitochondrion. Glycolysis pays in coins; respiration pays in notes.

## The frontier
*Inside the Cell · The Membrane Frontier*

### The Membrane

Two molecules thin, the membrane decides what enters and what leaves. Gates, pumps and receptors stud its surface — a customs office for a city of one.

**Speaker notes:** End of the inner tour. The membrane is the cell's border, customs office and sensory skin all at once.
